Relay is only as good as the outcome you give it. An outcome it can't verify is an
outcome it can't finish. This page is the practical guide to writing one.

## An outcome is a goal plus its checks

When you start a run, intake compiles your input into a <DocLink to="glossary" hash="seed">**seed**</DocLink> with three parts:

- the **outcome** — one verifiable statement of what "done" means;
- its **verifications** — the grounded checks that decide done-ness (at least one, and
  each must carry grounding);
- a **non-binding sketch** — optional orientation the orchestrator is free to ignore.

The first two are the contract. The sketch is only a hint.

## Make the outcome checkable

The test of a good outcome: could a critic that sees only the diff and some evidence
rule on it? If not, it's a vibe, not an outcome.

<CodeBlock
  lang="text"
  filename="not an outcome"
  code={`Refactor the auth module.
Make the dashboard faster.
Clean up the API.`}
/>

<CodeBlock
  lang="text"
  filename="verifiable outcomes"
  code={`Auth tests pass and no public API signature changed.
The dashboard's first paint is under 1s on the sample dataset, by the existing perf test.
Every endpoint in routes/ has a request-validation schema and the schema test passes.`}
/>

Each of the good ones names a state you can check and implies how to check it.

## Ground every check

A <DocLink to="glossary" hash="verification">verification</DocLink> carries a kind, the check itself, and **grounding** — why this check
proves the outcome. Grounding is required: intake rejects a seed whose check cites none,
and the critic rejects a verdict that cites no evidence.

Relay grades evidence cheapest-first — reach for the simplest check that actually proves
the point:

- **a command** that exits cleanly (a linter, a build);
- **a test** that passes;
- **an artifact or state assertion** (a file exists, a row is written);
- **a visual judgment** for UI outcomes (a screenshot graded at a chosen granularity);
- **a cross-provider review** of the diff;
- **a human gate**, for the irreversible or ambiguous call.

<Callout type="note" title="Prefer a command or a test">
  The cheapest grounded check that proves the outcome is the best one. A passing test is a sharper
  contract than a paragraph of description, and it's the kind of evidence the critic trusts most
  directly.
</Callout>

## Let the loop find the route

State the destination, not the turns. A good outcome says nothing about _how_ — which
files to touch, what order to work in. That is the orchestrator's job to decide and the
critic's to verify. If you find yourself writing steps, move them into the sketch, where
they stay non-binding, and keep the outcome about the end state.

## Two ways to seed a run

Interactive intake is the better path when the outcome is fuzzy — it asks just enough to
pin the checks down, then stops:

<CodeBlock lang="bash" code={`relay run`} />

Reach for `--outcome` when you already know exactly what "done" means; it compiles a
grounded seed from one line, no questions asked:

<CodeBlock
  lang="bash"
  code={`relay run --outcome "Auth tests pass and no public API signature changed"`}
/>
